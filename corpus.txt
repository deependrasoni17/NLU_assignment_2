ACADEMIC PROGRAMMES Rules & Regulations




Introduction

Academic programmes at the Indian Institute of Technology Jodhpur are designed to develop highly capable human resources who can understand emerging patterns of knowledge creation across disciplines, removing traditional boundaries between science, humanities, social sciences, and engineering. The institute aims to produce professionals who are able to address major societal challenges of the twenty first century such as energy, food, water, housing, mobility, and health. In addition to providing scientific knowledge, the institute also aims to inculcate human values such as courage, integrity, fairness, humility, and teamwork among its graduates through curricular, co curricular, and extra curricular activities on campus. Undergraduate academic programmes focus on developing a lifelong attitude toward learning, creative thinking, and exploration. Postgraduate academic programmes focus on developing deep subject knowledge combined with intellectual curiosity and the ability to solve new problems with an open and objective mindset.

The academic programmes are based on the semester system, the credit system, and the relative grading system. These programmes are administered by a duly constituted Academic Committee through the office of academics. The Academic Committee is chaired by the Dean Academics. In the absence of the Dean Academics, the Associate Dean Academics for postgraduate or undergraduate programmes discharges the responsibilities. The senate of the institute has formulated a set of rules and guidelines to maintain high academic standards and ensure smooth functioning of academic programmes. Within this framework, and subject to senate approval, different programmes may include additional academic requirements when necessary. The academic programmes are continuously monitored by the senate and modifications or improvements are made whenever required.

Academic Session

The academic session normally runs from the end of July in one year to the middle of July in the following year. The academic session is divided into three parts consisting of two regular semesters and a summer term. Semester one runs from the fourth week of July to the last week of November. Semester two runs from the last week of December to the last week of April. The summer term runs from the middle of May to the middle of July. Excluding examination days, the total number of instructional days in a semester is at least seventy.

Academic Calendar

The academic calendar specifies the exact dates of important academic events during the academic session. These events include orientation, registration, commencement of classes, addition and dropping of courses, submission of documents, examinations, submission of grades, project evaluation, declaration of results, mid semester recess, and vacations. The academic calendar is approved by the senate.

Registration

Students are required to register for prescribed credits according to their academic programme on the dates specified in the academic calendar. If a student fails to register on the scheduled registration date, registration can still be completed before the late registration deadline by paying a fine.

A new student joining the institute who is awaiting the final results of the qualifying examination may register provisionally by submitting a certificate from the previous institution confirming that the student has appeared for the final examination. The student must submit proof of passing the qualifying examination by the deadline specified in the academic calendar for the registration to become regular.

If a student fails to complete registration by the late registration deadline, the student will be deregistered. Appeals against deregistration may be submitted to the chairman of the senate through the Dean Academics. The chairman of the senate may permit continuation of registration upon payment of an appropriate fine.

Students may add or drop courses with approval from the faculty advisor until the final date specified for registration adjustments. After the add drop deadline, students may withdraw from a course. The withdrawal deadline normally occurs within four weeks from the beginning of the semester and is specified in the academic calendar. Withdrawn courses appear on the student grade card with the letter grade W.

Absence for four or more weeks during a semester results in automatic cancellation of registration for all courses in that semester.

Departments may offer summer courses to help students clear backlog courses or complete regular credit courses. A summer course may run provided a faculty member is available to conduct the course and the offering is approved by the Dean.

Teaching and Evaluation

Teaching

The medium of instruction at the institute is English.

All courses carry academic credits. Credits are based on the number of contact hours for lectures, tutorials, and practical sessions. Upon successful completion of a course with a passing grade, the student earns the corresponding number of credits. Courses are assigned different levels depending on the academic maturity required for participation. A course may consist of independent components that can be completed separately, referred to as fractals. Each course is identified by a unique course number, and the numbering system defines parameters related to course identification.

All courses and their associated credit weights must be approved by the senate. Only senate approved courses may be offered during any semester or summer term.

Before the beginning of each semester, the department responsible for the programme finalizes the list of courses to be offered.

Courses may be categorized as regular courses, self study courses, or independent study courses.

A regular course normally involves fourteen hours of classroom engagement for each credit. Regular courses may be either core or elective courses. Elective courses at levels one hundred through six hundred run when at least ten students register. Elective courses at levels seven hundred or eight hundred may run with a minimum of five registered students. Courses that do not run as regular courses may be offered as self study courses.

A self study course must be selected from the list of courses approved by the senate. A student may register for an elective course as a self study course when the course is not offered as a regular course in that semester or when the student is not present on campus, subject to approval from the competent authority. Students who previously failed a core course may also register for that course in self study mode if it is not offered during that semester.

Students applying for a self study course require recommendations from the course coordinator and the relevant academic committees. Final approval is granted by the Dean Academics. Formal classroom sessions may not occur for self study courses, but laboratory, design, or computational exercises may be conducted if they are essential to the course. Assessment is conducted by the course coordinator according to standard evaluation norms, and the approved course title appears on the transcript. No more than ten percent of the total graded credits may be taken through self study courses.

Independent study courses allow students to pursue academic topics of interest under the supervision of a faculty member. Independent study involves guided academic work rather than regular classroom instruction. Students must demonstrate self discipline and clear academic goals. Students with a cumulative grade point average of six or higher may enroll in an independent study course with recommendation from the supervising professor.

Students may register for an independent study topic for up to three credits once during the programme. Such courses count toward open elective requirements and appear on the transcript with the title Independent Study. Applications require recommendations from the course coordinator and the relevant academic committees, and final approval is given by the Dean Academics. Independent study courses generally do not include formal lectures but may include laboratory work, design tasks, or computational exercises when necessary. The supervising professor plans and manages the course, and assessment is conducted according to standard evaluation procedures.

Each course is conducted by an instructor with the assistance of tutors when required. The instructor is responsible for teaching the course, evaluating student performance, assigning grades at the end of the semester, and submitting grades according to academic procedures.

Students have the right to review their answer scripts for written examinations conducted in a course. Evaluation records must be maintained by the instructor for at least six months. Courses approved by the senate may be conducted through direct contact, synchronized remote contact, guided study mode, or a combination of these methods.

Direct contact mode involves face to face interaction between instructors and students. Synchronized remote contact involves simultaneous online interaction through electronic conferencing using audio and video communication, with a portion of the contact hours conducted through direct interaction. Guided study mode involves faculty guidance for self learning and discussion oriented sessions and may also allow students to study new courses under faculty supervision.

Formal evaluation components for courses offered through any mode are conducted under direct supervision of faculty members. Laboratory components requiring specialized facilities are conducted through direct contact.

Teaching assignments for instructors and tutors are allocated by the head of the department responsible for the programme. When faculty members from other departments participate in teaching a course, allocation is made in consultation with the head of the concerned department.

Students may audit a course in addition to their normal academic load with permission from the Dean Academics based on recommendations from the instructor and the faculty advisor. Audited courses appear on the student grade card.

Students are expected to maintain full attendance in each course. Unless leave is granted for valid reasons, students must attend all lectures, tutorials, and laboratory sessions. Attendance records must be accessible to students after each class. Even if attendance falls below seventy five percent, students may still appear for examinations. However, students with attendance below seventy five percent must score at least a C grade to pass the course, otherwise they receive a failing grade.

Evaluation

Student performance in a course is evaluated continuously through classroom interaction, examinations, laboratory work when applicable, term papers, and project work.

Minor one, minor two, and major examinations are mandatory components of evaluation for a regular fourteen week lecture course. Minor examinations normally last sixty minutes and the major examination lasts one hundred twenty minutes. Courses with fewer credits must include at least one examination.

The examination component normally contributes between forty percent and sixty percent of the total evaluation weightage. Evaluation policies must be communicated to students on the first day of class.

Continuous evaluation may include classroom participation, quizzes, assignments, tutorials, laboratory work, term papers, and project based assessments.


GRADING SYSTEM 



Letter Grades

At the end of the semester, a student is awarded a relative letter grade in each course by the instructor offering the course. The grade is determined by considering the performance of the student during the semester in comparison with the performance of other students registered in the course. Ten regular letter grades are used in the evaluation system. These grades include A star, A, A minus, B, B minus, C, C minus, D, E, and F. Each letter grade is associated with a numerical value on a ten point scale as defined in the institute grading system. In addition to the regular grades, four special grades are also used. These special grades include I for Incomplete, S for Satisfactory, X for Thesis Continuation, and U for Unsatisfactory. Faculty members are required to upload the grades on the official academic portal according to the stipulated schedule.

Procedure for Declaration of Results

The declaration of results follows a defined procedure and timeline according to the academic calendar of the institute. At the beginning of the semester, instructors display the evaluation scheme for their courses. During the semester and after examinations, instructors display answer sheets and the scores obtained by students. After reviewing all evaluation components, instructors finalize the grades. These grades are then reviewed by a department or institute level moderation committee in the case of department wide or institute wide courses.

After scrutiny by the moderation committee, the head of the department recommends the grades. Instructors then display the grades for students. If any correction is required, it must be completed within forty eight hours of the display of grades and must receive approval from the head of the department. After this step, the grades are approved by the Dean for undergraduate or postgraduate academics. The results are then finalized by the Dean and subsequently approved by the Chairman of the Senate. Once the results have been approved, they cannot be changed under any circumstances. In exceptional situations, a student may submit an appeal to the Chairman of the Senate for reconsideration with proper justification.

Grade Card and Transcript

At the end of each semester, a grade card is issued to every student. At the completion of the academic programme, a transcript is issued. The grade card and transcript issued by the institute indicate the courses completed at the institute along with the corresponding credits and grades. If a student has earned credits from another academic institution during a semester, the transcript may also indicate the total number of such credits without specifying grades.

Waiver of Requirements in Special Cases

The rules, procedures, and requirements described in the academic regulations manual may be waived in special circumstances except for those related to admissions to academic programmes. Such waivers may be granted by the Academic Committee based on the recommendation of the concerned department and with the approval of the Chairman of the Senate. All such exceptions must be formally reported to the Senate.

Make Up Examinations

Students who miss minor or major examinations due to genuine reasons such as illness may be permitted to take a make up examination for the missed component. The student must submit an application to the Dean Academics through the course instructor within ten days from the date of the missed examination explaining the reason for the absence. Applications submitted after this period may not be considered.

Permission for a make up examination is normally granted only in exceptional circumstances such as hospitalization due to illness. Students must provide appropriate documentation to support their request.

Students residing in hostels are required to produce a medical certificate issued by the medical officer of the institute confirming that the student was admitted to the hospital during the period of the missed examination. Students residing in hostels who consult doctors outside the institute must obtain written permission from the medical officer of the institute hospital before seeking such consultation. Students who receive treatment from external doctors must obtain an endorsement on the treatment certificate from the medical officer of the institute hospital.

Students residing outside the campus permanently or temporarily must provide a medical certificate issued by a registered medical practitioner. The certificate must also be endorsed by the parent or guardian.

A slot wise make up examination for major examinations will be conducted during the make up or supplementary examination week according to the academic calendar of the subsequent semester for students who are granted permission. If a student misses this make up examination, another opportunity is normally not provided. However, in exceptional cases such as prolonged illness, the Chairman of the Senate in consultation with the Dean Academics may permit the student to appear for the make up examination before the end of the first week of the next semester.

Under no circumstances is prorating permitted for missed examinations or attendance requirements.


REGULATIONS FOR POSTGRADUATE PROGRAMMES

Postgraduate Programmes

The Indian Institute of Technology Jodhpur currently offers several postgraduate programmes. These include the Master of Science programme, the dual degree Master of Science and Master of Technology programme, the Master of Technology programme, the dual degree Master of Technology and Doctor of Philosophy programme, and the Doctor of Philosophy programme.

Postgraduate Admissions

The admission process for programmes offered by the institute generally takes place during April and May for the first semester and during November and December for the second semester. In addition, departments may process applications for admission to Doctor of Philosophy programmes on a continuous basis and admit students according to the established procedures.

Categories of Admission

Candidates may be admitted to postgraduate programmes under several categories. These include full time regular, full time sponsored, part time, external, and executive categories. Not all categories are applicable to every postgraduate programme. Some programmes may also be offered through combinations of these categories, such as a fully sponsored part time programme.

A full time regular student is an in campus student who can register for the full credit load permitted per semester. Students supported through institute research projects are also considered full time regular students.

A full time sponsored student is an in campus student who can register for the full credit load per semester and whose studies are sponsored either by an employer or through self sponsorship. A self sponsored candidate must possess at least two years of relevant industrial, research and development, or academic experience.

A part time student is a working professional who attends regular classes while continuing employment. The student must be employed in the vicinity of the institute and must submit a no objection certificate from the employer to pursue the programme. A part time student may register for a limited number of credits compared with a full time student.

An external student is a candidate employed in an industry, research establishment, or academic institution that provides adequate research and library facilities. The employer must permit the student to spend the required residency period on campus.

The executive category applies to certain Master of Technology programmes. A professional in this category may complete the programme requirements in an accelerated manner within a minimum period of twelve months or may accumulate credits according to personal convenience and complete the programme within a maximum period of six years. The programme may be completed in part time or full time mode through synchronized remote contact courses along with limited direct interaction.

Eligibility for Admission

The eligibility conditions defined by the institute represent the minimum requirements for admission. Individual departments may specify additional requirements subject to approval by the senate. The number of admissions to each programme is determined by the senate from time to time. Reservation of seats for different categories is prescribed by the board of governors.

Admission to the Master of Science Programme

Admission to the Master of Science programmes offered by the institute may occur through two avenues. One avenue is through qualification in the Joint Admission Test for Masters conducted by the Indian Institutes of Technology. Another avenue allows applicants who hold a bachelor degree in a relevant discipline from a three or four year programme to apply through a written test or interview conducted by the institute.

Applicants under this second category must have a minimum of sixty percent marks for the general and other backward class categories and fifty five percent marks for the scheduled caste and scheduled tribe categories in aggregate, or a minimum cumulative grade point average of six point zero on a ten point scale for general and other backward class categories and five point five for scheduled caste and scheduled tribe categories. Equivalent proportional requirements apply when grading scales differ. Alternatively, a first class degree as specified by the awarding institution may also be accepted.

Admission to the Master of Science and Master of Technology Dual Degree Programme

Admission to the dual degree Master of Science and Master of Technology programme follows the same admission routes as the Master of Science programme. Applicants may qualify through the Joint Admission Test for Masters conducted by the Indian Institutes of Technology or may apply with a relevant bachelor degree and qualify through a written test or interview conducted by the institute.

Applicants must satisfy the minimum academic requirements including sixty percent marks or a cumulative grade point average of six point zero for general and other backward class categories and fifty five percent marks or a cumulative grade point average of five point five for scheduled caste and scheduled tribe categories. Equivalent proportional criteria apply for grading systems other than the ten point scale, or the applicant may hold a first class degree as specified by the university or institute.

Admission to the Master of Technology Programme

Applicants seeking admission to the Master of Technology programme must possess a bachelor degree in engineering or science from a four year programme or a master degree in science, computer applications, pharmacy, medical sciences, agricultural sciences, veterinary sciences, or another related discipline.

Applicants must have a minimum of sixty percent marks for general and other backward class categories or fifty five percent marks for scheduled caste and scheduled tribe categories in aggregate, or a minimum cumulative grade point average of six point zero for general and other backward class categories and five point five for scheduled caste and scheduled tribe categories on a ten point scale. Equivalent proportional criteria apply for other grading scales or the candidate may hold a first class degree as defined by the awarding institution.

Applicants must also possess a valid Graduate Aptitude Test in Engineering score, be exempted from the requirement according to relevant government regulations, qualify through an equivalent national examination, or pass the written test or interview conducted by the institute.

The main objective of the program is to create a pool of professionals with very strong fundamentals in the domain of physics. Based on the experience of teaching advanced physics for the past several years, the Department has evolved a unique combination of program structure, courses and teaching pedagogy which not only allows the students to acquire in depth learning of the concepts of physics and their application potential but also gives them freedom to choose the courses as per their interests from the large pool of elective courses being offered by the Department. These electives can be from three different sets of the courses namely advanced level fundamental physics, multidisciplinary science and technology and engineering physics.

The program gives strong emphasis on the experiential learning aspects. As many as four labs and two hands on courses are offered in the first year itself. In the hands on exercise the students are given some interesting problems of physics. These problems are designed in a way that students learn to apply the concept of physics, make use of innovative approaches and develop creative thinking. Such activities provide much needed practical learning which help the students to effectively comprehend the complex theoretical concepts of physics. After completion of the program the students would have acquired the knowledge and skill sets to pursue their career, as per their interest, in the directions like advanced level research in fundamental and applied physics, research and development in multidisciplinary science and technology, higher education in engineering domains or availing job opportunities in government, public and private sectors.

26. Rules and Regulations for Ph.D./M.Tech-Ph.D. Dual Degree Program

26.1 Thesis Supervisor(s)

26.1.1 Selection of Ph.D. Candidates

The selection should be based on merit and the capability of the candidate to conduct research. At least one faculty member must be available or likely to join in the areas of interest of the candidate at the time of selection.

26.1.2 Appointment of Thesis Supervisor(s)

Ph.D. students shall be admitted to the department and no supervisor will be assigned at the time of selection. Allotment of students to supervisors will be made based on the preferences of the students and supervisors. For both Ph.D. and M.Tech-Ph.D. dual degree programs, supervisor assignment and area identification must be completed by the end of the first semester. The Convener of the Departmental Research Committee or the Coordinator of the Interdisciplinary Research Program will advise the student until a regular supervisor is assigned.

Normally, there shall not be more than two supervisors for a student. An additional supervisor may be allowed with the approval of the Chairman of the Senate. A supervisor from outside the institute may be appointed before the qualifier stage upon request of the student or supervisor with the recommendation of the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee and approval of the Dean (Academics) and the Chairman of the Senate.

The external supervisor must sign Intellectual Property Rights and Non-Disclosure Agreement documents as part of the acceptance. From the financial perspective, the institute will have no obligation toward the external supervisor.

26.1.3 Caretaker Supervisor/Additional Supervisor

Whenever the supervisor proceeds on short leave for a period up to one year, the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee will appoint a caretaker supervisor in consultation with the supervisor and the student to take care of administrative requirements. Whenever the supervisor proceeds on long leave for a period exceeding one year, an additional supervisor must be appointed based on the suggestion of the supervisor.

A supervisor going on lien or sabbatical may continue supervising students and may opt for either an additional supervisor or a caretaker supervisor. A supervisor who retires or resigns may continue as a supervisor provided that the student has cleared the qualifier before the supervisor’s retirement or resignation, and an additional supervisor will be appointed if necessary.

26.1.4 Composition of Departmental Research Committee (DRC)

The Departmental Research Committee consists of coordinators of M.Tech, M.Tech-Ph.D. dual degree, and Ph.D. programs of the department, the Head of the Department, and a Senior Professor or Associate Professor proposed by the Head. The committee will be chaired by a Professor or Associate Professor nominated by the Head of the Department. The Chairman of the Departmental Research Committee will also be a member of the Academic Committee. The committee must be approved by the Dean (Academics). The Ph.D. program coordinator will serve as the convener of the committee and will be proposed by the Head of the Department. The tenure of the committee will be two years.

26.1.5 Composition of Interdisciplinary Research Committee (IRC)

The Interdisciplinary Research Committee consists of coordinators of all Interdisciplinary Research Programs and a Senior Professor or Associate Professor nominated by the Dean (Academics) as the chairman. The chairman of the Interdisciplinary Research Committee will also be a member of the Academic Committee. One of the coordinators of the interdisciplinary programs will serve as the convener. The tenure of the committee will be two years and the committee must be approved by the Dean (Academics).

26.2 Change of Research Supervisor

Change of supervisor under exceptional circumstances may be permitted by the Dean (Academics) upon recommendation of the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee after obtaining the consent of the student, the present supervisor, and the proposed supervisor. If required, realignment of the student’s research program will occur under the supervision of the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee.

26.3 Student Research Committee (SRC)

26.3.1 Composition of SRC

The Student Research Committee normally consists of the supervisor and three other faculty members familiar with the research field, with at least one member external to the department and possibly from outside the institute. The Student Research Committee will be proposed by the supervisor, recommended by the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee, and approved by the Dean (Academics).

The committee will have one member designated as Chairman for administrative functions while the supervisor acts as the convener. The Chairman of the Student Research Committee is appointed by the Dean (Academics). The formation of the Student Research Committee must be completed by the end of the first semester. Any subsequent changes in the composition of the committee will be communicated through the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee to the Dean (Academics) for record.

The Chairman and supervisor will serve as internal examiners for thesis evaluation. The Student Research Committee is responsible for monitoring the progress of the candidate until completion of the program.

26.4 Annual Progress Monitoring Meeting (APM)

The academic progress of all registered Ph.D. students will be reviewed once each year by the Student Research Committee and peers within the institute. The review will be based on the Annual Progress Report and a Poster Presentation open to all participants. The Student Research Committee will award either a Satisfactory or Non-Satisfactory grade based on these evaluations. Two consecutive Non-Satisfactory grades will lead to termination of registration.

For implementation convenience, the academic calendar will specify two days each year for conducting the annual progress evaluation of research scholars. Students joining in the first semester of the academic year will undergo evaluation between 1 July and 30 September. Students joining in the second semester will be evaluated between 1 December and 28 February.

The first Annual Progress Monitoring review will be conducted after successful completion of the qualifier requirements. Each semester, the supervisor will report either Satisfactory or Unsatisfactory progress through the Student Research Committee for record. Any exceptional situation will be processed by the Academic Committee and approved by the Dean (Academics).

26.5 Qualifier Requirements of Ph.D. Student

Four components together constitute the qualifier requirements for a Ph.D. student and eligibility for enhanced Senior Research Fellowship. These components include completion of course requirements with the minimum CGPA specified in Section 22.8.1, successful completion of the comprehensive examination, successful presentation on the state of the art of the chosen research field, and successful presentation and defense of the research proposal.

Ph.D. students are expected to complete the qualifier requirements before the beginning of the fifth semester of registration and in any case not later than the beginning of the sixth semester. Part-time candidates must complete the qualifier requirement before the beginning of the seventh semester.

26.5.1 Comprehensive Examination of Ph.D./M.Tech-Ph.D Student

The comprehensive examination is designed to test the general capability of the student and the breadth of knowledge in the discipline and related research areas. The examination is conducted by a Comprehensive Examination Committee of the Department or Interdisciplinary Research Program.

The examination consists of a written test with three papers. Departments will offer at least five papers for conducting the comprehensive examination, with each paper covering one essential sub-area of the department. The course content of these papers must be approved by the Academic Committee and the Senate. The syllabus for the comprehensive examination will not be identical to regular course offerings but will instead test the breadth of knowledge in the field.

The Student Research Committee specifies three papers as the comprehensive examination requirement for a particular student, and these papers may be selected from across departments. The comprehensive examination requirements must be communicated to the student within two weeks of formation of the Student Research Committee.

Comprehensive examinations are scheduled on two fixed days each year and may coincide with the annual progress evaluation days for doctoral candidates. Students may choose to appear for the three papers on the same day or on different days. A student may attempt the comprehensive examination before completing course requirements and achieving the minimum CGPA requirement.

If the performance of the student in any comprehensive examination paper during the first attempt is not satisfactory, the student will be given one additional opportunity to appear within six months of the first attempt. To clear the comprehensive examination, the student must perform satisfactorily in all papers.

The comprehensive examination must be completed within three semesters for Ph.D. students and within five semesters for Direct Ph.D. and M.Tech-Ph.D. dual degree students.

Laboratories

The Department of Physics places a strong emphasis on experiential learning as an integral part of its curriculum for MSc as well as Ph.D. students. This approach allows students to understand theoretical concepts more quickly and effectively through hands-on experience. The department therefore offers several laboratory courses for MSc students as prescribed in the program structure. The laboratory experiments are designed in such a way that students can comprehend the theoretical concepts taught in the classroom and develop the skill sets required to excel in their future professional endeavors.

Over the past few decades, there has been tremendous growth in computational capabilities. This development has significantly influenced research approaches not only in engineering domains but also in the core sciences. The use of advanced computational tools has made it possible to conduct experiments on virtual platforms through simulation and modeling processes. As a result, the pace at which research outputs are obtained has improved considerably. Today, many simulation and modeling outputs are directly applied in the design and development of systems and technologies. Consequently, the department ensures that computational laboratories receive appropriate emphasis alongside laboratories equipped with advanced experimental instruments.

Keeping these developments in view, the department has established several laboratories dedicated to both teaching and research activities. The teaching laboratories are equipped with specialized instruments that students can also use for their research work as part of their projects and thesis requirements.

In order to facilitate globally competitive cutting-edge research and the development of breakthrough technologies, it is essential to create an environment where students and faculty members have free access to research facilities not only within the department but also across all departments of the institute. With this philosophy, the department operates various thematic research laboratories where any student or faculty member can conduct research activities.

The department has established four focused research groups that conduct both fundamental and applied research. These research groups work in the areas of Quantum Physics, High Energy and Astrophysics and Optics and Photonics, and Condensed Matter and Plasma Physics. These groups are supported by thematic research laboratories that cater to the needs of faculty members, Ph.D. students, and research staff. Detailed information about the laboratories of the department, including teaching laboratories, research laboratories, thematic research laboratories, and research groups, is provided by the department. The laboratories are managed under the supervision of faculty members with the support of qualified technical staff and Ph.D. students.



Department of Mathematics

Mathematics is the foundation of many disciplines and continues to evolve over time by developing new theories that help solve real world problems. The Department of Mathematics at the institute plays an important role in developing new mathematical methods that can model complex situations arising in computer science, engineering, and basic sciences. The department includes faculty members with research interests in areas such as algebra, mathematical physics, scientific computation, numerical analysis, partial differential equations, topological dynamics, low dimensional chaos, dynamical systems, renormalization in low dimensional dynamics, wavelet analysis, fractional transform theory, image processing, financial risk analysis, categorical data analysis, reliability theory, and applied probability.

The department offers several high quality postgraduate programmes for students who wish to apply mathematics in science and engineering. These programmes include a two year Master of Science programme in Mathematics, a four year Master of Science and Master of Technology dual degree programme in Mathematics Data and Computational Sciences, and a two year Master of Technology programme in Data and Computational Sciences. The department also offers a Master of Technology and Doctor of Philosophy dual degree programme in Data and Computational Sciences and a Doctor of Philosophy programme with specialization in various areas of mathematics for students who wish to gain deeper knowledge in both pure and applied mathematics.

The Department of Mathematics is committed to advancing research and education in pure mathematics, applied mathematics, and statistics. The vision of the department emphasizes both the fundamental principles of core mathematics and the foundational concepts necessary for analyzing ideas used in emerging technologies. Since its establishment, the department has actively engaged in research across many important areas of mathematics and computing.

The department also focuses on exploring modern aspects of mathematics combined with computational capabilities to develop skilled professionals in mathematical aspects of emerging technologies. With this objective, the department has initiated academic programmes that address the mathematical needs of both industry and academia. The department currently offers a two year Master of Science programme in Mathematics, a four year Master of Science and Master of Technology dual degree programme in Mathematics Data and Computational Sciences, a two year Master of Technology programme in Data and Computational Sciences, an Integrated Teacher Education Programme with specialization in Mathematics, and a Doctor of Philosophy programme with specialization in mathematics, computing, and data and computational sciences. The department also offers a minor programme in Data Science for undergraduate students.

Faculty members in the department actively conduct research in several areas including algebra, complex analysis, topological dynamics, low dimensional chaos, renormalization in low dimensional dynamics, dynamics in neuroscience, mathematical physics, scientific computation, numerical analysis, optimization techniques, partial differential equations, wavelet analysis, fractional transform theory, image processing, financial risk analysis, categorical data analysis, reliability theory, financial mathematics, and applied probability. The department also maintains the required research infrastructure including high performance computing facilities.

25. Rules and Regulation for Conduct and Evaluation of M.Tech/M.Sc. Project

Allotment of students to supervisors will be made based on the preferences of the students and supervisors. The supervisor and area identification must be completed by the end of the first semester. The Coordinator of the M.Tech Program will advise the student until the student is assigned to a regular supervisor.

Each semester a letter grade will be awarded after examination by a committee consisting of the Departmental Research Committee, the supervisor, and two members conversant with the field of research with at least one member external to the department. The external member may also be from outside the institute. The Departmental Research Committee will appoint the examiners in consultation with the supervisor. The same committee may examine more than one thesis. The external member may participate through video conferencing. The Departmental Research Committee will ensure that the award of letter grades follows the relative grading policy.

A regular full time student must register for M.Tech Project Part-1 in the summer semester following the second semester. The student must obtain a satisfactory grade to continue the M.Tech Project Part-1 in the next semester. The student’s performance in the summer semester will be evaluated by the supervisor.

26. Rules and Regulations for Ph.D./M.Tech-Ph.D. Dual Degree Program

26.1 Thesis Supervisor(s)

26.1.1 Selection of Ph.D. Candidates

The selection should be based on merit and the capability of the candidate to conduct research. At least one faculty member must be available or likely to join in the areas of interest of the candidate at the time of selection.

26.1.2 Appointment of Thesis Supervisor(s)

Ph.D. students shall be admitted to the department and no supervisor will be assigned at the time of selection. Allotment of students to supervisors will be made based on the preferences of the students and supervisors. For both Ph.D. and M.Tech-Ph.D. dual degree programs, supervisor assignment and area identification must be completed by the end of the first semester. The Convener of the Departmental Research Committee or the Coordinator of the Interdisciplinary Research Program will advise the student until a regular supervisor is assigned.

Normally, there shall not be more than two supervisors for a student. An additional supervisor may be allowed with the approval of the Chairman of the Senate. A supervisor from outside the institute may be appointed before the qualifier stage upon request of the student or supervisor with the recommendation of the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee and approval of the Dean (Academics) and the Chairman of the Senate.

The external supervisor must sign Intellectual Property Rights and Non-Disclosure Agreement documents as part of the acceptance. From the financial perspective, the institute will have no obligation toward the external supervisor.

26.1.3 Caretaker Supervisor/Additional Supervisor

Whenever the supervisor proceeds on short leave for a period up to one year, the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee will appoint a caretaker supervisor in consultation with the supervisor and the student to take care of administrative requirements. Whenever the supervisor proceeds on long leave for a period exceeding one year, an additional supervisor must be appointed based on the suggestion of the supervisor.

A supervisor going on lien or sabbatical may continue supervising students and may opt for either an additional supervisor or a caretaker supervisor. A supervisor who retires or resigns may continue as a supervisor provided that the student has cleared the qualifier before the supervisor’s retirement or resignation, and an additional supervisor will be appointed if necessary.

26.1.4 Composition of Departmental Research Committee (DRC)

The Departmental Research Committee consists of coordinators of M.Tech, M.Tech-Ph.D. dual degree, and Ph.D. programs of the department, the Head of the Department, and a Senior Professor or Associate Professor proposed by the Head. The committee will be chaired by a Professor or Associate Professor nominated by the Head of the Department. The Chairman of the Departmental Research Committee will also be a member of the Academic Committee. The committee must be approved by the Dean (Academics). The Ph.D. program coordinator will serve as the convener of the committee and will be proposed by the Head of the Department. The tenure of the committee will be two years.

26.1.5 Composition of Interdisciplinary Research Committee (IRC)

The Interdisciplinary Research Committee consists of coordinators of all Interdisciplinary Research Programs and a Senior Professor or Associate Professor nominated by the Dean (Academics) as the chairman. The chairman of the Interdisciplinary Research Committee will also be a member of the Academic Committee. One of the coordinators of the interdisciplinary programs will serve as the convener. The tenure of the committee will be two years and the committee must be approved by the Dean (Academics).

26.2 Change of Research Supervisor

Change of supervisor under exceptional circumstances may be permitted by the Dean (Academics) upon recommendation of the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee after obtaining the consent of the student, the present supervisor, and the proposed supervisor. If required, realignment of the student’s research program will occur under the supervision of the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee.

26.3 Student Research Committee (SRC)

26.3.1 Composition of SRC

The Student Research Committee normally consists of the supervisor and three other faculty members familiar with the research field, with at least one member external to the department and possibly from outside the institute. The Student Research Committee will be proposed by the supervisor, recommended by the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee, and approved by the Dean (Academics).

The committee will have one member designated as Chairman for administrative functions while the supervisor acts as the convener. The Chairman of the Student Research Committee is appointed by the Dean (Academics). The formation of the Student Research Committee must be completed by the end of the first semester. Any subsequent changes in the composition of the committee will be communicated through the Departmental Research Committee or Interdisciplinary Research Committee to the Dean (Academics) for record.

The Chairman and supervisor will serve as internal examiners for thesis evaluation. The Student Research Committee is responsible for monitoring the progress of the candidate until completion of the program.

26.4 Annual Progress Monitoring Meeting (APM)

The academic progress of all registered Ph.D. students will be reviewed once each year by the Student Research Committee and peers within the institute. The review will be based on the Annual Progress Report and a Poster Presentation open to all participants. The Student Research Committee will award either a Satisfactory or Non-Satisfactory grade based on these evaluations. Two consecutive Non-Satisfactory grades will lead to termination of registration.

For implementation convenience, the academic calendar will specify two days each year for conducting the annual progress evaluation of research scholars. Students joining in the first semester of the academic year will undergo evaluation between 1 July and 30 September. Students joining in the second semester will be evaluated between 1 December and 28 February.

The first Annual Progress Monitoring review will be conducted after successful completion of the qualifier requirements. Each semester, the supervisor will report either Satisfactory or Unsatisfactory progress through the Student Research Committee for record. Any exceptional situation will be processed by the Academic Committee and approved by the Dean (Academics).

26.5 Qualifier Requirements of Ph.D. Student

Four components together constitute the qualifier requirements for a Ph.D. student and eligibility for enhanced Senior Research Fellowship. These components include completion of course requirements with the minimum CGPA specified in Section 22.8.1, successful completion of the comprehensive examination, successful presentation on the state of the art of the chosen research field, and successful presentation and defense of the research proposal.

Ph.D. students are expected to complete the qualifier requirements before the beginning of the fifth semester of registration and in any case not later than the beginning of the sixth semester. Part-time candidates must complete the qualifier requirement before the beginning of the seventh semester.

26.5.1 Comprehensive Examination of Ph.D./M.Tech-Ph.D Student

The comprehensive examination is designed to test the general capability of the student and the breadth of knowledge in the discipline and related research areas. The examination is conducted by a Comprehensive Examination Committee of the Department or Interdisciplinary Research Program.

The examination consists of a written test with three papers. Departments will offer at least five papers for conducting the comprehensive examination, with each paper covering one essential sub-area of the department. The course content of these papers must be approved by the Academic Committee and the Senate. The syllabus for the comprehensive examination will not be identical to regular course offerings but will instead test the breadth of knowledge in the field.

The Student Research Committee specifies three papers as the comprehensive examination requirement for a particular student, and these papers may be selected from across departments. The comprehensive examination requirements must be communicated to the student within two weeks of formation of the Student Research Committee.

Comprehensive examinations are scheduled on two fixed days each year and may coincide with the annual progress evaluation days for doctoral candidates. Students may choose to appear for the three papers on the same day or on different days. A student may attempt the comprehensive examination before completing course requirements and achieving the minimum CGPA requirement.

If the performance of the student in any comprehensive examination paper during the first attempt is not satisfactory, the student will be given one additional opportunity to appear within six months of the first attempt. To clear the comprehensive examination, the student must perform satisfactorily in all papers.

The comprehensive examination must be completed within three semesters for Ph.D. students and within five semesters for Direct Ph.D. and M.Tech-Ph.D. dual degree students.

Laboratories

The Department of Physics places a strong emphasis on experiential learning as an integral part of its curriculum for MSc as well as Ph.D. students. This approach allows students to understand theoretical concepts more quickly and effectively through hands-on experience. The department therefore offers several laboratory courses for MSc students as prescribed in the program structure. The laboratory experiments are designed in such a way that students can comprehend the theoretical concepts taught in the classroom and develop the skill sets required to excel in their future professional endeavors.

Over the past few decades, there has been tremendous growth in computational capabilities. This development has significantly influenced research approaches not only in engineering domains but also in the core sciences. The use of advanced computational tools has made it possible to conduct experiments on virtual platforms through simulation and modeling processes. As a result, the pace at which research outputs are obtained has improved considerably. Today, many simulation and modeling outputs are directly applied in the design and development of systems and technologies. Consequently, the department ensures that computational laboratories receive appropriate emphasis alongside laboratories equipped with advanced experimental instruments.

Keeping these developments in view, the department has established several laboratories dedicated to both teaching and research activities. The teaching laboratories are equipped with specialized instruments that students can also use for their research work as part of their projects and thesis requirements.

In order to facilitate globally competitive cutting-edge research and the development of breakthrough technologies, it is essential to create an environment where students and faculty members have free access to research facilities not only within the department but also across all departments of the institute. With this philosophy, the department operates various thematic research laboratories where any student or faculty member can conduct research activities.

The department has established four focused research groups that conduct both fundamental and applied research. These research groups work in the areas of Quantum Physics, High Energy and Astrophysics and Optics and Photonics, and Condensed Matter and Plasma Physics. These groups are supported by thematic research laboratories that cater to the needs of faculty members, Ph.D. students, and research staff. Detailed information about the laboratories of the department, including teaching laboratories, research laboratories, thematic research laboratories, and research groups, is provided by the department. The laboratories are managed under the supervision of faculty members with the support of qualified technical staff and Ph.D. students.

 Indian Institute of Technology Jodhpur – Comprehensive Overview
1. Introduction

The Indian Institute of Technology Jodhpur (IIT Jodhpur) is one of the premier engineering and technology institutions in India. Established in 2008 by the Government of India, the institute was created with the objective of expanding high-quality technical education and research in the country. IIT Jodhpur is part of the prestigious IIT system, which consists of autonomous public technical universities known for excellence in engineering, science, innovation, and interdisciplinary research.

From its inception, IIT Jodhpur has aimed to develop technological solutions that contribute to the economic and social development of India. The institute focuses on integrating teaching, research, and innovation to create impactful knowledge and technologies. The educational philosophy of IIT Jodhpur emphasizes scholarship in teaching and learning, research excellence, and strong engagement with industry.

Over the years, IIT Jodhpur has evolved into a vibrant academic community consisting of students, faculty members, researchers, and administrative staff who collaborate to create new knowledge and advance technology.

2. Establishment and Early Development

The establishment of IIT Jodhpur was part of a national initiative to expand the IIT system across India. In 2008, the Government of India announced several new IITs to meet the growing demand for world-class technical education. IIT Jodhpur was designated as the IIT for the state of Rajasthan.

The first academic session of the institute began in 2008 with undergraduate programs in Computer Science, Electrical Engineering, and Mechanical Engineering. During the initial phase, academic activities were conducted from the campus of IIT Kanpur. This arrangement provided mentorship and academic guidance to help the new institute establish its educational framework.

Soon after, IIT Jodhpur shifted its operations to a temporary campus in Jodhpur. This transit campus allowed the institute to expand its programs and develop its administrative and academic infrastructure while construction of the permanent campus progressed.

In 2017, the institute moved to its permanent residential campus located on the outskirts of Jodhpur.

3. Vision and Mission

IIT Jodhpur aims to become a global center for education, research, and innovation in science and technology. The institute’s mission revolves around several key principles:

Providing high-quality education in engineering, science, design, and management.

Promoting interdisciplinary research and technological innovation.

Developing solutions to societal challenges.

Building partnerships with industry, academia, and government.

Preparing students to become leaders and responsible global citizens.

The institute emphasizes three major driving forces:

Excellence in teaching and learning.

Excellence in research and creative accomplishments.

Relevance and engagement with industry.

These principles guide all academic and research activities at IIT Jodhpur.

The permanent campus of IIT Jodhpur spans approximately 852 acres of land located on National Highway 62 (Nagaur Road) north-northwest of the city of Jodhpur. Historical Context of the IIT System

The Indian Institutes of Technology represent one of the most respected networks of technical universities in the world. Their origin dates back to the early years after India gained independence, when national leaders realized the importance of advanced technical education for economic development and technological self-reliance. The government therefore initiated a plan to create institutions dedicated to engineering, scientific research, and technological innovation.

Over time, these institutes became globally recognized for producing highly skilled engineers, researchers, and entrepreneurs. Graduates from these institutions have contributed to major technological advancements in industries such as computing, telecommunications, aerospace, energy systems, and biotechnology.

The establishment of newer IITs during the 21st century was intended to extend these opportunities to more regions of the country. These new institutions were designed not only to replicate the success of older IITs but also to develop modern research agendas aligned with contemporary global challenges. IIT Jodhpur emerged during this expansion phase and was expected to bring advanced technological education to the western region of India.

From its earliest days, the institute adopted the same core values shared by the IIT system: academic freedom, rigorous scholarship, interdisciplinary research, and a strong commitment to innovation.

Joint Programs in Medical Technologies (IITJ & AIIMSJ): Creating a culture of innovation and entrepreneurship in an acad
Siddharth Srivastava
Scientific Social Connect


Imagine that on a Diwali eve, through all the revelry and flowing conversations, you were acutely aware of chest pain for the last three days, that refused to subside. However, with a history of acid reflux, recurring lung infections and a generous dose of wishful thinking, you avoided raising the alarm. No more, and the entire family got busy navigating the traffic, and labyrinths of hospital corridor while praying continuously. A doctor appeared who not just did ECG, hs troponin but also patiently asked about your family history, clinical information. It was indeed acid reflux and you trudged your way back to home. You filled the visitors and well-wishers with the harrowing stories of how you avoided last-minute disasters.

                              

Two years later, a friend phoned and narrated a similar episode of pain in the chest, however, with a very different process of triage. She had an affordable iHeart kit; the moment her chest pain grew worrisome, she pulled out a patch from the kit and pasted it over her neck covering her carotid artery. The data of her ECG, BP, pulse rate, pO2 was sent to the nearest emergency center. An embedded AI algorithm continuously monitored ECG patterns ready to relay the danger of myocardial infarction to the clinic. She pulled out the troponin strip from iHeart bag and with a simple prick and strip, she processed the image within her cellphone using an App provided by iHeart. An elevated ST in ECG was detected, along with an elevated troponin.

Flashing of these data spurred a remote control & consulting room into action. An ambulance was sent to patient's current location while a doctor made a call to the oblivious patient to calm her through the impending process. As soon as the patient consult got over the phone, she was carried to the nearest cardiac super specialty center where she got an angioplasty done on her.   Her experience was so seamless that she was talking more about the whole process to her visitors rather than the travails of getting an angioplasty. Her experience was designed and crafted to the T by physicians and engineers together, rather Physicianeers. So much was the boundaries blurred between their expertise that the doctor would explain the AI algorithms and the Engineer glance the ECG and mark the areas depicting cardiac conditions…and you wouldn't know who is who.



 

IITJ-AIIMSJ MedTech Center is home to deeptech innovators and entrepreneurs, who deliver solutions to patients/ physicians through startups.



The joint center has created a culture of innovation and entrepreneurship in an academic setting. This culture was created by faculty and students of the Joint MedTech program, around four ideas:

1. Diversity among students.

Diversity breeds innovation. Within the academic regulations, guide the student intake and shape a class through the philosophy that diversity in peer experiences, geography, and culture will accelerate disruptive thinking. Students of the Joint MedTech program are drawn from various engineering and medical disciplines, apart from four-year degree programs in sciences and pharmacy.

2. Identifying problems through immersion

Incorporate “problem identification through immersion” as an essential and major component of the curriculum. “Immersion” provides a comprehensive picture of the problem. A solution devised out of such an immersive experience is more likely to be accepted and utilized by stakeholders. Let students take ownership of problem identification and encourage/ guide their disruptive solution. For the joint program students, an elaborate semester-long clinical immersion is carried out where they have deep interactions with doctors, patients, visitors to AIIMSJ and its environment.

3. Link innovation to entrepreneurship. 

Entrepreneurship breeds further innovation beyond idea generation. It accelerates new innovations in building processes, business practices and policies to make a disruptive idea change lives. Students of joint MedTech program incubate their startups at TISC (IITJ incubator).

4. Academic experience laced with activities to practice innovation.

Innovation can be learned. Hackathons, ideation sessions, hands-on workshops on innovation, robust connections between research labs and incubators, and design thinking workshops will lead students to practice innovation in day-to-day life.

In four years of coming into being, the Joint Programs in Medical Technologies have given rise to 8 registered startups, 8 patents (submitted/ accepted), 4 BIRAC-BIG grants, 7 MSME grants, 8 DBT Biodesign fellowships, 5 research publications, 2 PMRF fellows. Copyright, Open Access, Open Licensing, & Open Educational Resources, Navigating Fair Use of Scholarly Resources
Kshema Prakash and Kamleshkumar J. Patel
Scientific Social Connect
In the realm of academia and research, the interplay between copyright, open access, open licensing, and open educational resources (OER) becomes a critical topic influencing the dissemination of knowledge and the accessibility of scholarly resources. As researchers, educators, and students navigate this landscape, understanding and abiding by the principles of FAIR use and Copyright Policy and its implications on the use of scholarly resources is paramount.

The relationship between copyright, open access, open licensing, and open educational resources (OER) is pivotal in academia and research, as it affects the availability of scholarly materials and the dissemination of knowledge. Researchers, educators, and students must comprehend the tenets of FAIR use policy and its implications regarding using scholarly resources as they traverse this terrain. 

 

Copyright and its Implications

What is copyright?

According to the World Intellectual Property Organization, “Copyright (or author’s right) is a legal term used to describe the rights that creators have over their literary and artistic works. Works covered by copyright range from books, music, paintings, sculpture, and films, to computer programs, databases, advertisements, maps, and technical drawings” (Copyright, n.d.).

 

What kind of intellectual works can be protected using Copyright?

The following intellectual works come under the ambit of copyright:

Literary works encompassing several forms such as novels, poems, plays, reference works, and newspaper articles;
Computer programs and databases;
Films, musical compositions, and choreography;
Artistic works encompass several forms such as paintings, drawings, photographs, and sculpture;
Architecture; and
Advertisements, maps, and technical drawings. (Copyright, n.d.)
 

Copyright law protects the creators’ exclusive rights to their intellectual and original work. In academic perspective, it can protect scholarly articles, books, and other academic materials. Basically, these rights encompass reproduction, distribution, creation of derivatives works.

In the academic sphere, researchers mostly rely on accessing and using copyrighted materials to enhance their knowledge and make valuable contributions to their respective fields. However, the traditional model of subscription-based access to scientific literature might pose difficulties in terms of accessibility and pricing, thereby restricting the spread of knowledge to individuals with institutional affiliations or financial resources.

 

Copyright implication on e-works

Subba Rao (2003) highlighted that copyright owners of e-works impose authorization on its use. One may acquire and retain a single copy for their personal use only and this kind of use explicitly prohibits copying or reproduction, distribution, republishing and selling. This applies to the context of subscribed scholarly resources, too.

Since the academic and research activities that drive the scientific publications, whose research is publicly funded, scholars worldwide have been concerned and have been working towards making knowledge freely accessible and free from the traditional restrictions.

 

Open Access, Open Educational Resources and Open Licensing

To address the access related challenges of the scholarly publications, the concept of Open Access emerged as a plausible solution for the scientific community. The concept of Open Access was developed in 1991 to understand the necessity to facilitate scholarly knowledge-based communication without restrictions. There are three major initiatives of Open Access, where major statements and declarations on the principles of Open Access were made on three international platforms, giving rise to the ‘BBB definition’ of Open Access. They are - Budapest OA Initiative, Bethesda Statement on OA Publishing, and Berlin Declaration on OA, as initiated in the developed countries (Ghosh & Das, 2007).

 

What is Open Access?

The term ‘Open Access’ is defined by Peter Suber as “Open-access (OA) literature is digital, online, free of charge, and free of most copyright and licensing restrictions. OA removes price barriers (subscriptions, licensing fees, pay-per-view fees) and permission barriers (most copyright and licensing restrictions)” (Peter Suber, Open Access Overview (Definition, Introduction), n.d.).


The latest definition of Open Access by Budapest Open Access Initiative says – 

By “open access” to [peer-reviewed research literature], we mean its free availability on the public internet, permitting any users to read, download, copy, distribute, print, search, or link to the full texts of these articles, crawl them for indexing, pass them as data to software, or use them for any other lawful purpose, without financial, legal, or technical barriers other than those inseparable from gaining access to the internet itself. The only constraint on reproduction and distribution, and the only role for copyright in this domain, should be to give authors control over the integrity of their work and the right to be properly acknowledged and cited. (BOAI10 – Budapest Open Access Initiative, n.d.)

 

What are Open Educational Resources (OER)?

The definition given by UNESCO is – “Open Educational Resources (OER) are teaching, learning and research materials in any medium – digital or otherwise – that reside in the public domain or have been released under an open license that permits no-cost access, use, adaptation and redistribution by others with no or limited restrictions. OER forms part of ‘Open Solutions’, alongside Free and Open Source software (FOSS), Open Access (OA), Open Data (OD) and crowdsourcing platforms.” (UNESCO, 2017)

 

What is Open Licensing?

Open License is defined by the Open Knowledge Foundation as “… a document that specifies what can and cannot be done with work (whether sound, text, image or multimedia). It grants permissions and states restrictions. Broadly speaking, an open license grants permission to access, re-use and redistribute a work with few or no restrictions” (Guide to Open Licensing - Open Definition - Defining Open in Open Data, Open Content and Open Knowledge, n.d.).

Creative Commons licenses provide everybody, i.e., from individual originators to large organizations, a uniform way to permit the public authority to use their artistic work under copyright law (“About CC Licenses,” n.d.). Creative Commons licenses have been commonly used in Open Access Publishing and Open Educational Resources. There are six flavours and attributions of the Creative Commons as mentioned in the Fig. 1 on How to Attribute Creative Commons (Foter Blog, n.d.).

The traditional copyright is also applicable for OER, but the nature of copyright is replaced with Open Licensing like Creative Commons with some restriction. Open Licenses have emerged to protect the creator rights in a digital environment where the contents can be easily shared and copied without the creator’s permission (Guidelines for Open Educational Resources (OER) in Higher Education, 2011).

Open Access publications and Open Educational Resources are typically available online without financial, legal, or technical barriers, allowing researchers, learners, and educators globally to access and use the scholarly contents and learning objects without restrictions. There are various Open Access models to publish the scholarly contents in journals providing contents on both paid as well as open access basis.



Fair Use of Scholarly Resources

While on the one hand is copyright, which is a right to protect the creator of their intellectual work from unauthorized use or unlawful replication, on the other hand are the open access and open educational resources (OER). Although open access and OER provide viable alternatives to traditional subscription-based models, many researchers still depend on access to subscribed journal resources for their work. In such situations, fair use provisions within copyright law might be applicable, permitting limited use of copyrighted materials without requiring permission from the copyright holder.

The Indian Copyright Act, 1957, the Section 52(1) allows fair use of copyrighted work for research, teaching and learning purposes only, and not permissible for commercial use (Copyright Act, 1957, 1957). Please refer to the details of “Fair use exceptions for libraries under Indian Copyright Law” at BananaIP® (Reporter, 2017).

When considering fair use, it is important to take into account factors such as the purpose and character of the use, the nature of the copyrighted work, the amount and substantiality of the portion used, and the effect of the use on the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work. Researchers should be careful and follow fair use guidelines when using subscribed journal resources for activities like criticism, commentary, research, and teaching.

Since a majority of the scholarly resources are published by global publishers, most of the contracts are governed by U.S. Copyright Law, or others as may be the case of applicability. However, libraries always indicate and inform the DO’s and DON’Ts of accessing scholarly resources to their users during their interactions, like orientation programs, focused group discussions, reference interviews, etc. Besides these, the libraries also conduct training sessions on fair and judicious use of scholarly resources subscribed them for their users. The highlights of such instructions are, as below:

 

The users are expected to honour and adhere to the copyright laws that rule the publishers/ consortia, and follow the copyright restrictions. Care should be taken not copy/ publish/ disseminate/ display/ perform, any copyrighted resource/ material, without obtaining prior permission from the copyright holder except when in accordance with fair use or licensed agreement.

Similarly, the journal resources are bound by ‘Licensing Restrictions’. Using these resources for other than the purposes of research, teaching, and private study, shall be construed as unlawful. Therefore, the users are expected to refrain from systematic downloading, copying or distributing of information, for commercial purpose or any other purposes.

Having discussed about copyright and fair use of scholarly resources, it is only natural to wind up the topic with plagiarism prevention. In simple words, plagiarism is using and presenting someone else’s ideas, work, writing, research etc. for their own purpose, and it is an offense. Therefore, a good practice would be to give credibility to the work of the original creators with proper citations. Libraries provide access to a range of writing support tools like reference managers, citation generators, and plagiarism detection software. It is always good to run the work for a plag-check for any intentional or unintentional plagiarism.

 

Conclusion

The convergence of copyright, open access, open licensing, and open educational resources carries important implications for researchers, educators, and students alike. Copyright laws provide legal protection for creators, while open access and OER initiatives encourage equal access to knowledge and encourage collaboration and innovation within the academic community. Understanding copyright regulations and practicing responsible scholarly behaviour are essential, especially, when using paid journal resources. By adopting the principles of transparency and equitable utilization, researchers can help create a more accessible and inclusive academic environment for future generations.

One step towards Calmness
The Technique:
Progressive Muscular Relaxation (PMR) is a medically proven technique to sooth and calm your nerves. Well-known for it’s ease , this user friendly exercise can take care of anxiety and it’s resultant fall-outs.

How does PMR work:
PMR prompts you to enhance the awareness of your own bodily reactions and changes in tense/ worrying situations. This follows the learning to, first releases muscular tension and finally calming down / soothing of mind. You achieve this by following a series of simple verbal instructions about small muscles, with eyes closed and concentrating on own body. PMR can be done seating or lying down position and ends with deep relaxation of body. It does not take more than 30 minutes of PMR for you to experience calming effect and mental peace. A good PMR session underlines the principal of Mind-Body Relatedness; which shows that we can unlearn the reflexive habits of tension/ anxiety responses to stressful situations by re-instructing our mind and body. The relaxed state of mind and body, achieved through PMR learning, leaves it’s positive effect on our health, with better self-control and preparedness to face tensions in life. The ease of instructions can help you to master it on your own with regular practice. As there are no copyrights for this, you can help your friends and family members too, after mastering yourself.

Where can it help you:
Most of those situations when you become very tense, worry too much, anticipate bad outcomes such as exam tensions, anxiety/ headaches before viva/ interview, stage fright, speaking in front of class, strangers/ opposite gender and so forth. PMR is also beneficial in improving disturbed sleep, poor appetite/ digestion, fatigue, hypertensions , ulcers and other problems having origins with mental anxiety and emotional disturbances.

Learning Progressive Muscular Relaxation (PMR) is a positive step towards healthy life style. Take the first step…..
